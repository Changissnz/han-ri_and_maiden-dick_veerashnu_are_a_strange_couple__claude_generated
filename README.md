# Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick Veerashnu are a Strange Couple
A tale about a strange couple and their tenant in a weird America. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.

One peculiar thing about Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick is that no one could really identify their ethnicity. Some say they are the species of Tarkatan, from the fictional Mortal Kombat video game series. But that is just farfetched. The truth was probably more mundane and more complicated simultaneously — the kind of ethnic ambiguity that America produces when it imports the world and then declines to organize it into anything coherent. People looked at Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick and reached for the nearest available category, found none that fit, and settled on staring.

Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick could speak multiple languages — Farsi, Hindi, Mandarin, Japanese, Tagalog, and Vietnamese. This was not a party trick. It was the accumulated residue of lives lived across contexts, of having needed to communicate with too many different kinds of people to afford the luxury of monolingualism. In America, this made them unusual. In most of the world, it would have made them simply functional.

The couple were not without their blemishes. Han-Ri, based on some of his features, seemed to have been afflicted by another person's hand. Maiden-Dick seemed stressed and tense all the time. These were not things they discussed. They were simply present, the way certain histories are present — visible if you knew what to look for, unremarked upon by everyone who had learned that some things are better left in the category of understood without being said.

Even so, the couple were, for the most part, nice. However, the nature of American politics was with them. That nature probably still is with them.

And the nature of American politics followed some predictable principles. Vassal states, protectionism, trade wars, instigation of ethnic strife. Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick had watched this nature operate across enough geographies and enough decades to recognize it on sight. They had not come to America with illusions. They had come with languages and a clear-eyed sense of what empires do — how they expand, how they manage the populations they absorb, how they sustain themselves less through unity than through the careful maintenance of division.

America had taken up the mantle of the British empire in this regard, priding itself more on maritime trade than on domestic strength of unity, unlike the German model, which had historically been more army-based during its imperial structure. Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick understood this distinction with the precision of people who had been on the receiving end of both models and had developed, out of necessity, a sophisticated taxonomy of imperial behavior.

They rented out a room in their home because it was practical. Most decisions in their life were practical.

The man who took the room was named Jan.

Jan is a mixed breed, and acted, for the most part, like a brute bastard. But a brute bastard on an assignment. Jan is apparently an informant, like many people in his shoes. It was the only way to get by in the diverse ethnic flora that America had let in, but not been able to adequately support without inciting blood. He had found his niche in the gap between official surveillance and deniable action — the gray space that empires always maintain and always deny maintaining.

Jan was supposed to keep an eye on another woman, by the name of Montagua Darovich. Montagua is a bastard. She was abandoned when she was young, and was comprised of three ethnicities: French, Vietnamese, and Russian. This combination had given her a face that confused people in the same way Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick's faces confused people, and a temperament that had been forged in the specific furnace of being nobody's obvious constituency — not French enough, not Vietnamese enough, not Russian enough, and therefore required to construct herself from scratch, which she had done, and the result was a free spirit in the truest sense of the term: not carefree, but genuinely unencumbered, because she had never been given anything to be encumbered by.

Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick, the geopolitically connected actors that they were, disapproved of Jan's stalkerish behavior. The couple knew who Montagua Darovich was. The couple, because of their multiculturalism, sympathized with the hardships of women such as Montagua Darovich. They had seen this particular dynamic before — the apparatus of surveillance turned toward women who existed outside protected categories, women whose ambiguity made them available targets precisely because no one with institutional power was obviously obligated to care about them.

The couple suspected Jan did not have noble interests in keeping an eye on Montagua Darovich. Bear in mind, the nature of American politics is very capital-intensive. Information about people was a commodity, and Jan was a man who moved commodities. What he did with them afterward was his own business, and that was the part that troubled Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick most.

It is true that Jan had some unusual fantasies about Montagua Darovich. He had been dating a French woman during his reconnaissance of Montagua. The French woman was very thick, much thicker than the voluptuous PAWGs. She was also, in the way of people who are present and available and real, increasingly insufficient for what Jan had decided he wanted.

Jan had become more and more fed up with dating the French woman. This, in turn, motivated him to pursue Montagua through unnatural and aggressive means. The logic was not exactly rational, but it had its own internal consistency — the French woman was what he had; Montagua was what he had constructed in her absence; and the gap between the two had become, in his mind, the French woman's fault.

What he planned to do was sell out Montagua's information to the military industrial complex. Then he would consummate with her, and then find a way to make money off of her activity. It was, in its way, a complete plan — surveillance, intimacy, and profit arranged in sequence, which is not so different from how empires have always operated, only smaller and more personal.

Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick correctly predicted that this was the strategy. They had seen enough of the world to read the shape of a man's intentions from his behavior, the way you can read weather from the way animals move before a storm. They discussed it between themselves in one of their six languages, which gave the conversation a privacy that their thin walls could not otherwise have provided.

One day, they decided to have a conversation with Jan's French girlfriend about Montagua. After some facts were mentioned, the French girlfriend threw a major fit.

The major fit resulted in Jan punching the girlfriend in the face. The police were called, and Jan feigned mental illness. Jan spent three weeks in the mental hospital, and broke up with his girlfriend. He was also kicked out of the rental of Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick.

---

Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick watched him go with the specific expression of people who had hoped for a better outcome and were not surprised by this one. They had done what they could do. In the architecture of American politics — with its vassal states and its trade wars and its carefully maintained ethnic tensions — what they could do was never quite enough, but it was also never nothing, and they had learned to live in that gap with something that was not quite peace but was close enough to it.

Maiden-Dick made tea. Han-Ri sat with whatever was visible in his features and did not discuss it. Outside, the country continued doing what it did.

---

Jan did not care. He knew now that Montagua Darovich fit his definition of love at first sight. There was no one that could interfere in his pursuit of her anymore. Not the strange Tarkatan couple Han-Ri and Maiden-Dick. Not his obese French ex-girlfriend. There were only two people in this world that mattered to him at that point. Himself. And Montagua Darovich.

He walked out into the American afternoon with the clean, terrible focus of a man who has simplified his world down to a single object and called it love.

Montagua Darovich, free spirit, bastard child of three nations, did not yet know his name.
